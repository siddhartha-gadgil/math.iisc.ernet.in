Development of Advanced Computational Algorithms for Real Time Optimal Control

Research Group

Radhakant Padhi and Soumyendu Raha

Aerospace Engineering, IISc and Supercomputer Education and Research Centre, IISc


Many challenging real life problems can be formulated in the framework of optimal control theory. However the optimal control formulations usually lead to two-point boundary value problems, which in turn lead to large computational requirements. Moreover, the control solution becomes 'open loop', which is highly undesirable. Hence, optimal control theory is rarely used in online applications. However, many methods are available for off-line solutions, which can be classified as either direct or indirect methods. The direct methods obtain the control solution by directly minimizing the objective function, subject to the constraints of the problem [Betts (2001)]. A major disadvantage of these methods is that they usually result in large optimization problems, and hence, are computationally inefficient. Indirect methods are based on the calculus of variations and the Pontryagin's maximum principle [Bryson and Ho (1975)]. Such formulations lead to two point boundary value problems, the solution of which requires numerical techniques like 'gradient method'[Kirk (1970)], 'shooting method' [Roberts and Shipman (1972)] etc., which are computationally intensive.


A promising approach for obtaining state feedback solution of general nonlinear optimal control problems is dynamic programming, which leads to the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation [Bryson and Ho (1975)]. However, the HJB equation is a nonlinear partial differential equation, which is very difficult to solve symbolically. Attempts to solve it numerically, on the other hand, gets trapped in the "curse-of-dimensionality" issue, which requires huge (infeasible) computational requirements. To avoid this, an alternative idea called 'approximate dynamic programming (ADP)', followed by dual heuristic programming has been proposed in the literature [Werbos (1992)]. This idea essentially facilitates a dual network adaptive critic (AC) approach, where a neural network captures the optimal control solution (in a state feedback form) in the domain of interest [Han and Balakrishnan (2002)]. A neural network approach, however, suffers from the drawbacks like difficulties in selecting and training the networks in a consistent manner, analytical proofs for stability and optimality etc. More important, if any part of the system trajectory falls outside the domain for which the networks are trained, the control prediction may be quite erroneous from the optimal value.


Another very promising idea that has successfully been applied in the industry (especially in process control applications) is model predictive control (MPC) [Rossiter (2003)]. In this method, based on the error between predicted and desired outputs, an appropriate cost function is minimized, which facilitate the control solution. This process is iterated upon with a sliding time window. Since the prediction horizon can be adjusted by the control designer, the computational complexity is much lesser as compared to a true finite-time optimal control problem. Hence, it is often found feasible to implement the MPC technique in many real-time applications, especially for slow-varying systems (like chemical engineering problems). However, since it does not take into account full operating time window, the control solution is usually suboptimal. Moreover, it cannot be applied to fast varying systems such as problems in aerospace engineering.


In this research, the motivation is to develop very fast computational algorithms for a variety of challenging problems, so that the solution can actually be implemented in real-time and hence can serve as a tool for online trajectory optimization. This is expected to be a 'game changer' with huge benefits like successfully preplanning the trajectories of aerospace vehicles online, thereby avoiding potential mission failures and aborted missions. A key focus will be on problems with differential and algebraic constraints (including state and control inequality constraints) so that the solutions can be of good practical usage. Application problems will largely be from the domain of aerospace engineering, where such problems are truly challenging. Good application problems from other areas (such as biomedical engineering) can also be considered if the student is interested in that field.
