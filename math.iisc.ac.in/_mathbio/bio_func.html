Towards understanding the role of molecular diffusion in biological function

Research Group

Anand Srivastava and Deepak Nair

Molecular Biophysics Unit, IISc and Centre for Neuroscience, IISc

In the last few years single particle tracking methods using localization based super resolution microscopy such as photoactivated localization microscopy (PALM), stochastic optical reconstruction microscopy (STORM) and fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (FCS) has become pivotal tools to understand unperturbed macromolecular behavior. These techniques can achieve high specificity in macromolecular labeling and much-improved spatial and temporal resolution. Most of the macromolecular mobility experiments are performed at a temporal resolution of few microseconds to millisecond, which is much higher than the intramolecular behavior with a time scale of few nanoseconds. Also, the single molecule trajectory experimental read-out is assumed to be Brownian dynamics in nature and the localization and tracking is carried out accordingly. Moreover, majority of the trajectories are obtained as 2-dimensional data (projected due to limitations in 3D microscopy) and extracting accurate diffusion behavior is non-trivial, particularly for membrane-proteins where membrane curvature and local membrane heterogeneity and undulations need to be factored in. Very recent papers have proposed conflicting models for protein diffusion on/in membrane ranging from Saffman-Delbruck to Stokes-Einstein like behavior. Researchers are not even able to converge on one single definition of hydrodynamic radius, though there are now theoretical models that forgo the assumption of any correction on solute size (hydrodynamic radius) to predict diffusion constant.


A combined experimental-theoretical approach is needed to develop a data-driven unified theory that can be used to interpret single-particle tracking data from experiment with higher fidelity. To an extent, experimentally guided higher resolution molecular modeling work in Dr. Anand Srivastava lab (MBU) can fill the dynamics gaps in fluorescence-microscopy and single-molecule spectroscopy data and his methods can be used to monitor short-term dynamic coupling between different components in the biological system including curvature and local chemical heterogeneity effects. Carefully designed single-particle microscopy experiments (under different boundary conditions) in Dr. Deepak Nair’s (CNeS) lab will provide a rich set of tracking data, which will be used to develop and validate the theory that can explain the diffusion behavior accurately. The theory developed from model experimental systems will be used to rigorously interpret new experimental data on real biological systems for scientific understanding in synaptic biology or structural organization of lipids in high-curvature membrane remodeling processes such as cell-motility, endocytosis and viral fusion.
